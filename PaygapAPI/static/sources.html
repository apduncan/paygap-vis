Sources
Gender pay gap service
gender-pay-gap.service.gov.uk

A majority of the data came from the government's gender pay gap reporting serivce. Data was published as it was gathered, so small amounts were available before the official early April deadline.

Companies were required to report several statistics about differences in overall pay between men and women, such as the difference in pay between the average man and woman.

Companies House
beta.companieshouse.gov.uk

The Companies House API was used to find detail about the current directors of companies, and which areas the company works in.

This was limitted to companies which had provided a valid company number in the gender pay gap reporting. Several types of organisations, such a co-operative or public sector bodies, do not report to companies house, so similar details for them is not available.

genderize.io
genderize.io

Genderize.io provides a freely available API which will attempt to return the likely gender of a forename. Each company director retrieved from Companies House was gendered using genderize.io, and a percentage of female directors generated.

In some case genderize is unable to predicate a gender for a forename. When the gender of one or more directors for a company is unknown, the percentage of female directors has been left blank.

Office of National Statistics
ons.gov.uk

Some companies reported one or more Standard Industrial Classification (SIC) code, indicating which fields they operate in. The ONS was used to provide a heirarchical structure for these SIC codes, which are grouped at different levels. Some organisations, mostly public sector, did not report any SIC codes and they could not be scraped from Companies House. These have all been grouped into 'Unreported'.

The average weekly pay for each SIC grouping was also added into the database. The ONS gives these estimates levels of confidence, however this uncertainty information is not represented in these visualisations.

Measures
Mean Pay Gap

From the gender pay gap service.

This is calculated by first finding the mean pay of female employees, and the mean pay of male employees. The mean pay gap is the difference between the two expressed as a percentage of mean male pay.

A mean pay gap of 10% would show that the mean pay for women is lower than the mean pay for men, by 10% of the mean pay for men. If the mean pay for men was £100, the mean pay for women would be £90

Instructions from the government on calculating these figures can be found here

Median Pay Gap

From the gender pay gap service.

This is calculated by first finding the median pay of female employees, and the median pay of male employees. The median pay gap is the difference between the two expressed as a percentage of median male pay.

A median pay gap of 10% would show that the median pay for women is lower than the median pay for men, by 10% of the median pay for men. If the median pay for men was £100, the median pay for women would be £90

Instructions from the government on calculating these figures can be found here

Mean Bonus Pay Gap

From the gender pay gap service.

This is the difference in bonuses paid to male and female employees. Where no employees were paid a bonus, this measure is blank, and will not appear on their profile or in aggregate measures.

This is calculated by first finding the mean bonus pay of female employees, and the mean bonus pay of male employees. The mean bonus pay gap is the difference between the two expressed as a percentage of mean male bonus pay.

A mean bonus pay gap of 10% would show that the mean bonus pay for women is lower than the mean bonus pay for men, by 10% of the mean pay for men. If the mean bonus pay for men was £100, the mean bonus pay for women would be £90

Workforce Female

From the gender pay gap service.

Organisations were asked to divide their workforce into quartiles by pay, the top quartile being the 25% of their employees who were paid the most. They reported what percentage of each quartile was female, and the overall percentage of their workforce who are female is derived from this.

Quartile Skew

Derived from the gender pay gap service.

Quartile skew represents to what extent women are likely to be the lower pay bands for this organisation. A high quartile skew suggests a greater proportion of women in the organisation are in the lower pay quartiles, and fewer in the higher quartiles. A negative quartile skew suggests the opposite, than women are more likely to be in the higher pay quartiles than lower.

Organisations were asked to divide their workforce into quartiles by pay, the top quartile being the 25% of their employees who were paid the most. They reported what percentage of each quartile was female.

The calculation of skew is taken from this page, and the population skew formula used. Each quartile is treated as being made up of 100 data points, and women in that quartile assigned value 1, 2, 3, or 4 depending on quartile

Female Directors

Derived from Companies House and genderize.io

The percentage of this companies directors who are female, as determined by genderize.io

If there is one or more directors whose gender is unknown, the measure is left blank for that company.

Genderize.io returns a probability of the gender being correct for each name, however this uncertainty has not been represented in these visualisations.

Sector Mean Weekly Pay

From Office of National Statistics

The estimated mean weekly pay of this SIC grouping.

Where an organisation has not reported any SIC codes, this is not available.

The majority of organisations which did not report any SIC codes were public sector, such as NHS Trusts and local government.
